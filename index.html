About

My research asks how economic statistics and welfare criteria should represent well-being when prices, constraints, and preferences are changing, with a particular interest in how the climate transition will affect inequality.

Research interests

Welfare economics; measurement of well-being, inequality and real income; consumer behavior and preferences; climate transition and living standards.

Working paper

Will Standard-of-Living Indicators Capture the Welfare Effects of Climate Transition Policies?

Didier Blanchet and Craig Pesme

A shorter version was published in Economie et Statistique / Economics and Statistics, 543, 3-20, 2024.

Abstract

Climate policies are designed to reduce future well-being losses from climate change, but they may impose short and medium-run costs on households. This article asks whether standard measures of living standards will accurately record these costs as the transition unfolds. We use a stylized model to compare the evolution of standard indicators with consumer utility under three transition mechanisms: green creative destruction, taxation of carbon-intensive goods, and quantitative restrictions on their consumption. Price-mediated mechanisms are, in principle, largely captured by existing indicators, although new-good effects and non-homothetic preferences generate familiar index-number problems. However, regulatory constraints can reduce welfare without changing either nominal income or observed prices, and can therefore leave measured real income unchanged. We then discuss how equivalent-income methods can be used to monetize such constraints and to incorporate non-monetary co-benefits such as health or environmental quality. Finally, we discuss how the welfare interpretation of chain-weighted real income can partly survive endogenous greening of preferences, while becoming more path-dependent and normatively more fragile.

Working paper

Inequality Aversion and the Measurement of Real Income

Craig Pesme

Abstract

Under non-homothetic preferences, the measurement of real income is not ethically innocuous. Money-metric utility and related welfare indices require a choice of reference prices, and different admissible choices can change conclusions about growth comparisons, inflation inequality, and aggregate welfare. This paper studies how individual welfare should be cardinalized, and how social welfare should be evaluated, when that normalization choice itself affects the degree of inequality aversion embodied in the social ranking. The paper proposes an axiomatic approach in which inequality aversion is made primitive through a transfer principle based on tolerated leakage in equalizing income transfers. The tolerated leakage is summarized by a concave function φ, yielding the notion of φ-prioritarianism. Together with standard axioms, it characterizes a canonical notion of real income and a canonical social ranking. Under additional conditions, the latter can be written as a prioritarian sum of money-metric utilities with an endogenously selected reference price system. The paper then derives explicit formulas for standard non-homothetic demand systems, including Gorman polar form, PIGL, and non-homothetic CES, and shows how the resulting welfare measure can be expressed as a correction to Divisia-style growth indices. Finally, the analysis is extended to heterogeneous preferences, clarifying what can and cannot be recovered once interpersonal comparability must also be addressed.

Working paper

Fair Interpersonal Comparisons: Characterizing Contour-Set Volume Measures of Well-Being

Craig Pesme

Abstract

How can welfare be compared across individuals with heterogeneous preferences without imposing arbitrary interpersonal utility normalizations? A situation is a pair (x, R), consisting of a bundle and a preference relation. Building on the contour-based approach to fairness and well-being measurement, the paper first characterizes the relevant information in a situation by its opportunity-equivalent profile: the collection of indices of nested opportunity sets whose best attainable element is just indifferent to the individual’s actual bundle. The main question of the paper is then whether such contour-based well-being measures can be neutral with respect to commodity labels and units of measurement. It is shown that these neutrality requirements are incompatible with Pareto on unrestricted domains of continuous, convex, and monotone preferences. Positive results therefore require common restrictions on the geometry of admissible preferences. Under such restrictions, and conditional on a separability axiom of contour information, neutrality characterizes a family of volume-type well-being measures. These measures evaluate a situation by the weighted volume of the relevant lower or upper contour set. They can therefore be interpreted as a continuous generalization of Borda-count ranking methods: they count the alternatives dominated by the individual’s actual bundle. For CES preferences, the representation yields a geometrically normalized utility index.
